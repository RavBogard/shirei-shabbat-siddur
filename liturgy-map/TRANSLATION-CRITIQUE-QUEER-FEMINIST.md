## The lenses

- **Feminist liturgical critique** — Judith Plaskow, *Standing Again at Sinai* (1990): God-language shapes community; women must be restored to Jewish memory as subjects, not additions `[high]`. Marcia Falk, *The Book of Blessings* (1996): the sovereign/king metaphor system is not neutral — hierarchy itself, not just grammatical gender, is the theological problem `[high]`. Rachel Adler, *Engendering Judaism* (1998): covenant re-imagined through lover and partnership metaphors rather than dominance `[high]`. Phyllis Trible's observation that *rachamim* (compassion) shares its root with *rechem* (womb) `[high]`.
- **Queer liturgical practice** — *Siddur Sha'ar Zahav* (2009), the landmark LGBTQ congregational siddur `[high]`; Joy Ladin, *The Soul of the Stranger* (2018): a God who transcends embodiment and category as a trans-resonant theology `[high]`; Elliot Wolfson's studies of gender and eros in Kabbalah (relevant to L'cha Dodi and Yedid Nefesh) `[high that this scholarship exists and is central; med on any specific formulation`]; Noam Sienna, *A Rainbow Thread* (2019), for the long queer Jewish textual record `[high]`.
- **Womanist/expansive midrash** — Wilda Gafney, *Womanist Midrash* (2017), directly relevant to Bilhah and Zilpah `[high]`.

## 1 · What the translation already achieves (name it before critiquing it)

These are not small things; several put this siddur ahead of the major movement siddurim.

1. **The sovereign-metaphor system is largely dismantled, deliberately and consistently.** *Melech ha'olam* → "Presence of the universe" / "Source of all being"; *Baruch shem k'vod malchuto* → "Blessed is the radiant Name whose glory is forever" (no kingdom at all); Ten Days *melech chafetz bachayim* → "You who delight in life." This is the Falk program executed inside a traditional Hebrew text rather than by rewriting the Hebrew — arguably a stronger move than Falk's own, since the full traditional text stays singable. The book is even self-aware about its one retained majesty (the *haMelech hakadosh* footnote: "the one image of majesty the High Holy Days keep even where the rest of this siddur sets it aside").
2. **Bilhah and Zilpah, named, with a footnote that says why** ("the enslaved women whose children became four of the twelve tribes but whom the tradition long left unnamed. To speak their names is to insist that the covenant runs through them too"). This is the strongest feminist gesture in the book and it is exactly what Gafney's womanist midrash asks liturgy to do: not just add the honored women, but the unfree ones.
3. **The kimsós chatán al kalá degendering.** L'cha Dodi v.7 ("as a bridegroom rejoices over a bride") is rendered "as one who is glad rejoices over a beloved." A quietly radical, queer-inclusive translation choice already made — a same-gender couple can stand inside this line. Flagged below (§2.1) only because it interacts with the bride imagery kept elsewhere.
4. **"whoever you love" on the welcome page.** Explicit queer inclusion at the book's threshold, in Daniel's voice, before a word of liturgy.
5. **Miriam credited at the sea** (Emet v'Emunah footnote: "the song Moses and Miriam sang"); **Debbie Friedman and Drorah Setel credited by name** in the Mi Shebeirach footnote — a feminist liturgical lineage acknowledged as *lineage*, which almost no siddur does.
6. **Psalm 29 *b'nei elim*** → "children of the mighty" (not "sons"); **Yedid Nefesh *ben ahuvecha*** → "the beloved child."
7. **The soul as "she."** Yedid Nefesh keeps *nafshi*'s grammatical femininity alive in English ("heal her by letting her glimpse the sweetness of Your light") — the reader briefly prays in a feminine voice regardless of their own gender. Wolfson's work shows how much of kabbalistic prayer runs on exactly this gender fluidity; the translation preserves it where most English versions flatten it.

## 2 · Findings, with options

Each row: what the text does · the reading through these lenses · options. **Recommendation marked ➤ where I have one; every call is Daniel's.**

### 2.1 The bride/queen complex (Yedid Nefesh → L'cha Dodi → captions → ritual mark M2)

**Text:** Shabbat is "the bride," "Shabbat herself," "Shabbat the queen"; the section opener says the Safed mystics "walked into the fields at sunset to greet Shabbat as a bride"; ritual mark M2 reads *some bow to greet Shabbat the bride*; the L'cha Dodi caption: "Shabbat the bride is arriving now."

**Readings.** Two legitimate, opposed feminist readings — the book should choose knowingly rather than by default:
- *Critique:* the frame is heteronormative at the structural level — the (implicitly male) congregation as groom, the feminine as the awaited, welcomed, veiled arrival. The feminine enters the liturgy as *object of greeting*, not agent (Plaskow's core structural point) `[high as a reading in the literature]`.
- *Defense:* the bride is one of the very few *female-coded divine images* the tradition itself preserves (Shabbat/Shekhinah as the feminine of God), and feminist liturgists have wanted it amplified, not removed; queer readers of Kabbalah (Wolfson) note the mystics themselves occupied shifting gender positions in this drama — male kabbalists singing as the bride's lovers *and* as the bride. Removing her would be a feminist loss `[high]`.

**Options.**
- (a) ➤ **Keep the bride entire, and let a footnote carry the depth.** One commentary footnote at L'cha Dodi noting that Shabbat-as-bride is the tradition's female image of the divine, and that the mystics who wrote it moved between gender positions as they sang it. This keeps the image and *queers it honestly* — the footnote-as-Torah convention is built for exactly this. (Draft on request.)
- (b) Keep the text as is, no addition (the kimsós line already does quiet work).
- (c) Vary the epithets in captions/kavanot ("Shabbat herself," "the queen," "the beloved") to loosen the single bride frame. Cheap, but dilutes the poem's own climax (*bo'i chalá*).

### 2.2 Residual "Sovereign/Master/dominion" — a consistency pass, not a policy change

**Text.** The de-sovereigning is deliberate but not quite complete. Remaining instances of the register the book elsewhere retired: Psalm 95 "a Sovereign above all powers"; Aleinu "the Sovereign beyond all sovereigns… In truth our Sovereign… The Eternal shall be Sovereign over all the earth"; Ya'aleh V'yavo "a gracious and compassionate God and Sovereign"; **Adon Olam: "Eternal Master, who reigned… then were You called Sovereign… Yours are strength and dominion."**

**Reading.** Falk's point is about the *metaphor system*, and a system half-retired reads as inconsistency rather than intention `[high]`. Aleinu is arguably a knowing exception (it *is* the coronation prayer — the footnote could say so, as the haMelech hakadosh footnote already does for the Ten Days). Adon Olam has no such structural excuse: "Master" and "dominion" are the hymn's medieval court furniture, and the rest of this book has already shown it can translate around such furniture without breaking meter or meaning.

**Options.**
- (a) ➤ **Treat Aleinu as the marked, intentional exception (one clause added to its existing footnote), and soften Adon Olam:** "Eternal Master" → "Eternal One" (the unit title already says exactly this); "Yours are strength and dominion" → e.g. "Yours are the strength and the majesty"; "then were You called Sovereign" can stand (it reports a naming, not a claim). Psalm 95/Ya'aleh V'yavo: small case-by-case swaps ("above all powers" already does the work in Ps 95 — "a Sovereign" could be "great beyond all powers").
- (b) Leave everything; accept a mixed register.
- (c) Full sweep to zero sovereignty language — against the book's own stated intent (the High Holy Days exception) and Daniel's "ease off particularism, don't cut the Hebrew" instinct; not recommended.

### 2.3 Psalm 97: *b'not Yehudá* — "the towns of Judah"

**Text:** "Zion hears and is glad, the towns of Judah rejoice."

**Reading.** The Hebrew says *the daughters of Judah rejoice*. "Towns" is a scholarly-respectable metonymic reading, but this book does not usually choose the reading that removes women from the page — it restored Bilhah and Zilpah two sections later. A rare self-inconsistency, easy to fix `[high that both readings are lexically defensible]`.

**Options.** (a) ➤ "the daughters of Judah rejoice." (b) Keep "towns," note nothing. (c) "the daughters of Judah — her towns — rejoice" (both; heavier).

### 2.4 *Rachamim* and the womb — an enrichment opportunity, not a defect

**Text.** "Compassionate Source" (Yedid Nefesh), "source of mercy, who in compassion remembers Your creatures for life" (Ten Days), "have compassion on us" (Ya'aleh V'yavo), G'vurot "with great compassion."

**Reading.** The tradition's own deepest feminine God-language is hiding in plain sight: *rachamim* shares its root with *rechem*, womb — compassion as womb-feeling (Trible) `[high]`. The book loves exactly this kind of etymological footnote (see *vayinafash* "re-souled," *l'takken olam*).

**Options.** (a) ➤ One footnote, once, at G'vurot or Yedid Nefesh: compassion's Hebrew root is the womb; every plea for rachamim asks God to feel toward us as a womb feels toward what it carries. (Draft on request.) (b) Skip.

### 2.5 The Purim Al HaNisim: *taf v'nashim* and the gallows

**Text:** "young and old, children and women, in a single day… and he and his sons were hanged upon the gallows."

**Reading.** Two flinches through a feminist lens: women listed among the plunderable categories (that is Haman's genocidal inventory, i.e., the *villain's* gaze — defensible as quotation), and the prayer's celebratory close on collective execution. Esther, the story's agent, appears only in the dateline. This is the traditional text, and the standing principle is not to cut Hebrew `[settled]` — so the lever here is only the footnote, which currently reads the prayer as "a threatened people and a reversal of fortune."

**Options.** (a) ➤ Extend the existing footnote by one sentence naming Esther's agency (the reversal runs through a woman's risk — "if I perish, I perish") and letting the gallows sit as the text's own hard edge rather than ours. (b) Leave as is — the footnote already de-triumphalizes. (c) Nothing.

### 2.6 "Fathers and mothers," always in that order

**Text.** *Avoteinu v'imoteinu* throughout → "our fathers and mothers," every time. The one exception is the book's own Mi Shebeirach, whose chiasmus ("blessed our fathers, Source of blessing for our mothers — blessed our mothers, Source of blessing for our fathers") is the Friedman/Setel text.

**Reading.** The Hebrew fixes the order; the English needn't, every time. Minor — but "mothers and fathers" alternating in the *translations* (not the transliterations) would cost nothing and quietly enact what the Avot v'Imahot footnote preaches `[low-stakes]`.

**Options.** (a) Alternate order in the English only, unit by unit. (b) ➤ Leave uniform (matching translit/Hebrew has real pedagogical value in a transliteration-forward siddur — a reader tracking word-by-word is better served by parallel order). Genuinely balanced; listed because a reviewer *will* someday ask.

### 2.7 Welcome page — one possible widening

**Text:** "whatever you believe or doubt, whatever your background, whoever you love, whether you have prayed a thousand Friday nights or none."

**Reading.** "Whoever you love" reaches sexual orientation; nothing yet reaches gender identity. Ladin's point: the deepest trans resonance in Jewish theology is a God who is never adequately embodied or named — which this book's whole translation practice already enacts. Making the welcome explicit is a style call, not a theological one `[high]`.

**Options.** (a) Add one clause — e.g. "…whoever you love, however you name yourself…" (b) ➤ Leave — the sentence's rhythm is Daniel's, already approved, and the translation practice itself is the statement. Flagged so the choice is conscious.

### 2.8 Chosenness (scope note, carried from the log)

The printed Kiddush and Festival Kiddush translate *asher bachar banu mikol am* straight: "who chose us from among all peoples… You chose us from among all peoples." Not strictly a queer/feminist item — but it is the one deferred translation-phase decision this read-through found still unmade, so recording it here so it doesn't slip. ## 3 · What was checked and found sound (so no one re-litigates it)

- **Sh'ma & V'ahavta:** English second person is naturally ungendered; "your God" throughout; no issue.
- **Hashkivenu / Shechinah:** the Avodah footnote already names the Shechinah as "the indwelling Presence" — the feminine divine is present where the tradition put her.
- **Me'ein Sheva:** "let all Israel… find rest in her" — Shabbat's grammatical femininity kept, matching §1.7.
- **Aleinu particularism:** kept-with-teaching-note per Daniel's 2026-07-04 ruling; the footnote's "particular calling, not superiority" is the standard liberal reading and reads honestly. Not reopened.
- **Mourner's Kaddish widening** (*v'al kol yosh'vei tevel*) — the universalizing move is made in the Hebrew itself and footnoted; exemplary.
- **G'vurot double text** (*m'chayeh hakol/meitim* side by side) — doubt-friendly, body-affirming, already the book's own best argument that it can hold two truths on one line, which is the same muscle §2.1(a) asks for.
