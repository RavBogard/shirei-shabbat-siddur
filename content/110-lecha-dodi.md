# L'cha Dodi · לְכָה דוֹדִי

## kavanah
A wedding poem by the mystics of Safed. They would dress in white and walk out to the fields to greet Shabbat like a bride, as the sun set over the mountains.

## hebrew

## translit
### Refrain
Lecha dodi likrat kallah, p'nei Shabbat n'kab'la.
### Shamor v'zachor
Shamor v'zachor b'dibur echad, hishmi'anu El ham'yuchad.
Adonai echad u'shmo echad, l'shem u'l'tif'eret v'li't'hilah.    (L'cha…)
### Likrat Shabbat
Likrat Shabbat l'chu v'nelcha, ki hi m'kor hab'racha.
Merosh mikedem n'sucha, sof ma'aseh b'machashava t'chila.    (L'cha…)
### Hit'oreri
Hit'oreri, hit'oreri, ki va orech kumi ori.
Uri, uri, shir dabeiri, k'vod Adonai alayich nigla.    (L'cha…)
### Yamin usmol
Yamin usmol tif'rotzi, v'et Adonai ta'aritzi.
Al yad ish ben Partzi, v'nismecha v'nagilah.    (L'cha…)
### Bo'i v'shalom
Bo'i v'shalom ateret ba'ala, gam b'simcha uv'tzahala.
Toch emunei am segula, bo'i chala, bo'i chala.    (L'cha…)

## translation
**Refrain:** *Come, beloved, to greet the bride — let's welcome the face of Shabbat.*

**Shamor v'zachor:** *"Protect" and "remember" — one word, one breath — the One let us hear it. HaShem is one, and the Name is one — for naming, for splendor, for praise.*

**Likrat Shabbat:** *Come, let's go toward Shabbat — she is the source of blessing. Poured out from time's beginning: last to be made, first to be imagined.*

**Hit'oreri:** *Wake up, wake up — your light has come — rise, shine. Awake, awake — break into song — HaShem's radiance breaks open on you.*

**Yamin usmol:** *Right and left you will burst through; it is HaShem you will adore. Through the one who breaks through — we will rejoice, we will be glad.*

**Bo'i v'shalom:** *Come in peace, crown of her beloved — with joy, with jubilation. Into the faithful, the treasured people — come, bride; come, bride.*

## commentary
- °*L'cha Dodi* was written by Shlomo HaLevi Alkabetz in 16th-century Safed, where Kabbalists would walk into the fields at dusk on Friday to greet Shabbat as a bride. The poem is an acrostic: the first letters of its verses spell out שלמה הלוי, the poet signing his own name inside the prayer. *[spike sample note — for Daniel's review]*
- °The refrain's *dodi* — "my beloved" — is borrowed straight from the Song of Songs. Kabbalat Shabbat takes the Bible's most unabashed love poetry and points it at time itself: Shabbat as the beloved we've been waiting all week to meet. *[spike sample note — for Daniel's review]*
